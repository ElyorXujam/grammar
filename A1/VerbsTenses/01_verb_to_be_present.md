# English Grammar Core: Verb "to be" (Present Tense)

### Quick Overview
The verb **"to be"** is the most fundamental building block in English. It connects subjects to descriptions, statuses, or identities. In the present tense, it splits into three distinct forms: **am**, **is**, and **are**.

---

### Structural Breakdown

#### 1. Affirmative (Positive)
* **I** $ightarrow$ **am** (`I'm`) $ightarrow$ *I am eager to expand my professional network.*
* **He / She / It** $ightarrow$ **is** (`He's / She's / It's`) $ightarrow$ *She is incredibly sharp and resourceful.*
* **You / We / They** $ightarrow$ **are** (`You're / We're / They're`) $ightarrow$ *They are fully aware of the project requirements.*

#### 2. Negative
Simply append **not** right after the verb. In natural conversation, contractions are highly preferred.
* *I **am not** (I'm not) available for a call at the moment.*
* *He **is not** (isn't) convinced by the current proposal.*
* *We **are not** (aren't) prepared to make a final decision yet.*

#### 3. Questions
Invert the order of the subject and the verb **"to be"**. Never use auxiliary verbs like *do* or *does* here.
* ***Are you** satisfied with the current layout?*
* ***Is he** the main point of contact for this account?*

---

### Noticeable Feedback & Common Pitfalls
* ⚠️ **The Double Subject Error:** Avoid repeating the subject when using a pronoun. 
    * *Incorrect:* `My manager she is coming.` 
    * *Correct:* `My manager is coming.`
* 💡 **B2 Context:** Use contractions (`isn't`, `aren't`, `I'm`) to sound natural and fluid. Leaving them uncontracted makes your speech sound overly rigid or robotic, unless you are deliberately emphasizing a point.
