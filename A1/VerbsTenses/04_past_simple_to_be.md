# English Grammar Core: Past Simple of "to be"

### Quick Overview
Unlike every other verb in the English language, **"to be"** retains two distinct forms in the past simple: **was** and **were**. Choosing the correct one depends entirely on whether your subject is singular or plural.

---

### Structural Breakdown

| Subject Type | Form | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Singular** (*I, He, She, It*) | **was** | *I **was** responsible for the system deployment.*<br>*It **was** a seamless transition.* |
| **Plural / You** (*You, We, They*) | **were** | *We **were** thrilled with the initial feedback.*<br>*They **were** absent from the briefing.* |

#### Negatives & Questions
* *Negative:* *She **wasn't** (was not) aware of the deadline expansion.* / *They **weren't** (were not) ready.*
* *Question:* ***Were** you present during the final wrap-up meeting yesterday?*

---

### Noticeable Feedback & Common Pitfalls
* ⚠️ **The "You Were" Mistake:** Even though "you" can refer to a single person, it **always** takes the plural verb form **were**. 
    * *Incorrect:* `You was very helpful yesterday.`
    * *Correct:* `You **were** very helpful yesterday.`
* 💡 **Collective Nouns Accent Nuance:** * In US English, collective groups are singular: *The team **was** unanimous.*
    * In UK English, they are often plural: *The team **were** celebrating.* * Both are correct, but consistency is key.
