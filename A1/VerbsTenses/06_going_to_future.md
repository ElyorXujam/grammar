# English Grammar Core: "Going to" for Future Intentions

### Quick Overview
We leverage **"going to"** when discussing **future plans, intentions, or firm choices** made *prior* to the moment of speaking. It indicates that the path forward has already been considered or calculated.

---

### Structural Breakdown
$$	ext{Subject} + 	ext{am/is/are} + 	ext{going to} + 	ext{Base Verb}$$

* **Positive:** *We **are going to re-evaluate** our budget parameters next fiscal quarter.*
* **Negative:** *He **is not going to sign** the agreement until his attorney reviews it.*
* **Question:** ***Are you going to submit** your report before the weekend?*

---

### Dual Functionality

#### 1. Prior Intentions (Pre-planned)
The decision happened before speaking.
* *I **am going to enroll** in an advanced data analytics course next month.*

#### 2. Predictions Based on Present Evidence
You can visually or logically see the outcome coming based on current circumstances.
* *The framework load is spiking; the application **is going to crash** if we don't scale up.*

---

### Noticeable Feedback & Common Pitfalls
* ⚠️ **Spoken vs. Written Form:** In colloquial speech, "going to" sounds like *"gonna"*. While perfectly fine for spoken practice, **never** write "gonna" in professional text, documentation, or emails.
* ⚠️ **Skipping the Auxiliary:** Always pair it with the correct present form of *to be*.
    * *Incorrect:* `I going to fix this.`
    * *Correct:* `I **am** going to fix this.`
